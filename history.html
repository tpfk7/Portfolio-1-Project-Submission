Creating The Colosseum


Almost two thousand years ago the infamous roman emperor Nero took his own life plunging Rome into chaos. In just one year after Nero’s death four different emperors had taken to the throne. The fourth however, Vespain, would end up ruling for 10 years followed by his sons and Domitian would be known as the Flavian emperors. In the year 70 AD Vespain took the lush land in the center of Rome where Nero had a palace and returned it to the people. He decided that on that piece of land he would erect an amphitheater where the public could go to view gladiator games and other forms of entertainment. (for free) After ten years of construction Vespians son Titus officially opened the Colosseum. The Colosseum opened with 100 days of games. The Colosseum, as well as the handling of the Vesuvius disaster cemented Titus as a well loved ruler. It was his brother however, Domitian, who would oversee the final construction of the Colosseum.


The Centerpiece of Rome


The largest amphitheater in the Roman empire, the Colosseum was (and still is) a site to behold. Unlike many other amphitheaters at the time that were built into a hillside for support the Colosseum was freestanding. It had a total of about 80 arched entrances supported by large semi-circular columns. The structure was three stories tall with columns of a different style on each story. 50,000 people could be seated in the Colosseum, most likely arranged by social class. Though it is thought the Colosseum would have been tightly packed it also had its comforts. During a particularly hot day awnings would be unfurled to protect the spectators from the hot sun while they watched a variety of entertainment including gladiator fights, animal hunts, animal combat, executions and even mock naval battles in which the Colosseum would be flooded with water. The Colosseum through Time


The Colosseum was used for almost 400 years. With new struggles in the western Roman empire and a change in public interests the costly Colosseum put an end to most of its events by the 6th century AD. The structure had also suffered damage due to natural disasters over that time such as lightening and earthquakes. As time went on the Colosseum was no longer a marvel of Rome and was used a quarry for multiple buildings such as Cathedrals and fortifications. It wasn’t until the 18th century that a strong desire to preserve the Colosseum began to arise mostly from the different popes who thought the site should be preserved as a monument to Christian martyrs. Though the theory of early christians being killed in the Colosseum has been called into question in recent years. As time went on the Colosseum was hit with more natural disasters, vandalism and weathering. Nearly two thirds of the original Colosseum had been destroyed but in the 1990s restoration efforts began and have continued.
